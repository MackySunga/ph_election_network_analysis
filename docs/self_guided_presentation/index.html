Online Attention, Network Centrality, and Electoral Outcomes

A web-ready interactive presentation of Philippine Election 2025 Twitter/X discourse as a network science study.

Prepared by Bob Mathew D. Sunga, MSc

How to read this app

This version is written so it can be uploaded to a web server and still make sense without a live presenter. Each slide includes the visual, the interpretation, and the reason the result matters.

Core idea: candidates, hashtags, and users are not isolated items. They form a political communication system where position, connection, and community structure matter.

Research Problem

Can online political networks reflect actual election outcomes?

The project studies whether Twitter/X election discourse behaves like a measurable communication network, and whether that network aligns with Senate vote outcomes.

The 2025 Philippine Senate election generated large volumes of Twitter/X discourse. Instead of treating those posts as isolated messages, this study treats them as connected traces of public attention: candidates mentioned together, hashtags used together, users replying or mentioning others, and topics attaching to political actors.

The goal is not to claim that Twitter/X directly predicts elections. The goal is to test whether online visibility, network centrality, and discourse position reflect actual electoral performance.

Research questions

  • Which candidates and hashtags became structurally central?
  • Which nodes connected separate discourse communities?
  • Did online network prominence align with actual vote rank?
  • Which candidates were overrepresented or underrepresented online?

Why this matters

The datasets are linked through candidates. Twitter/X produces online visibility measures; the Senate dataset provides the actual vote outcome used for comparison.

Important caution

Twitter/X is not the electorate. It is a platform sample shaped by users, media, controversy, and campaign activity. That is why the study focuses on alignment, not prediction.

Network Science Framework

Nodes, edges, weights, centrality, and communities

These are the concepts used to interpret the election discourse as a complex communication structure.

Node

A candidate, hashtag, user, or topic that appears in the discourse.

Edge

A relationship, such as co-mentioned candidates or co-occurring hashtags.

Weight

The strength of a relationship, usually based on frequency.

Centrality

How structurally important a node is inside the network.

Bridge

A node that connects different parts of the discourse.

Community

A cluster of nodes that are more connected to each other than to the rest of the network.

Interactive Network 1

Candidate Co-Mention Network

Candidates are connected when they appeared in the same tweet. Size shows PageRank. Thicker lines show stronger co-mention relationships.

How to read this graph

Each node is a Senate candidate. A line means two candidates were discussed together in the same tweet. This does not automatically mean support; it means discourse association.

Hover or click a node. Look for large central candidates and thick edges such as Bam Aquino–Kiko Pangilinan, Bong Go–Bato Dela Rosa, and Ben Tulfo–Erwin Tulfo.
Takeaway: co-mention networks reveal discourse clusters and candidate relationships, not just candidate popularity.

Interactive Network 2

Hashtag Co-Occurrence Network

Hashtags are connected when they appeared in the same tweet. The graph reveals how election topics and media labels connected.

How to read this graph

Each node is a hashtag. Lines show co-use. Large central nodes act as discourse hubs, while bridge hashtags connect different hashtag communities.

#halalan2025 should appear as the main connective hub. #eleksyon2025 and #comelec help structure the public election conversation.
Takeaway: important hashtags are not only frequent; they can also connect otherwise separate conversations.

Interactive Network 3

Candidate–Hashtag Bipartite Network

Candidates connect to hashtags used with them. This visualizes the discourse ecosystem surrounding political actors.

How to read this graph

Blue nodes are candidates. Yellow nodes are hashtags. A line means a candidate appeared in tweets using that hashtag.

Use this graph to see which candidates share hashtags, which hashtags cross candidate communities, and which candidates have broad discourse ecosystems.
Takeaway: this is the strongest network science visual because it links political actors to discourse markers.

Outcome Alignment

Network metrics strongly aligned with actual votes

The strongest relationship was found between actual votes and online visibility/network embeddedness.

Key interpretation

Mention count, co-mention weighted degree, unique hashtags, view count, and PageRank showed strong positive alignment with actual vote totals.

These are not merely popularity metrics. Co-mention weighted degree and PageRank describe how embedded a candidate is in the political communication network.

This is network evidence: structural embeddedness mattered more than simple viral engagement alone.

Outcome Comparison Simulator

Online rank is related to vote rank, but not identical

This scatterplot compares Twitter/X mention rank against actual Senate vote rank. Lower ranks are better on both axes.

How to read it

Points near the diagonal suggest online-vote alignment. Points far from the diagonal reveal mismatch.

A candidate can be highly visible online without winning nationally. A candidate can also win despite being less dominant on Twitter/X.

Online Overperformance

Who was louder online than electorally?

This slide shows the gap between online mention rank and actual vote rank.

What this reveals

Positive values indicate candidates who ranked better online than in actual votes. Negative values indicate candidates who performed better electorally than their Twitter/X visibility suggested.

This distinction is crucial because online attention may come from controversy, activist communities, media cycles, or platform-specific demographics.

The chart separates online loudness from electoral strength.

Limitations

What this project does not claim

The project is intentionally careful: it studies online political communication, not the full voting population.

This project does not claim that Twitter/X directly predicts elections. Online discourse is affected by platform demographics, controversy, campaigns, media activity, and the visibility of politically engaged communities.

The dataset captures one platform and one form of public expression. It does not measure private voting decisions, offline campaign machinery, regional political networks, or non-Twitter/X voter blocs.

Safe claim

Twitter/X network centrality is a strong signal of online political attention and electoral visibility, but it is not a complete proxy for national voter behavior.

This is why the study reports alignment, overrepresentation, and underrepresentation rather than claiming perfect prediction.

Conclusion

The election discourse behaved like a complex network

Philippine Election 2025 Twitter/X discourse formed connected networks of candidates, hashtags, users, and topics. Centrality measures and candidate–hashtag relationships strongly aligned with actual Senate vote outcomes, while also exposing important mismatches between online visibility and electoral strength.

For web visitors viewing this without a presenter, the key lesson is this: Twitter/X does not simply show who is popular. It shows how political attention is connected, amplified, clustered, and sometimes distorted by the platform environment.

Twitter/X is not a direct election predictor. It is a powerful network lens for studying political attention, discourse structure, and communication influence.
